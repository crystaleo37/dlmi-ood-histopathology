\begin{abstract}
We address out-of-distribution (OOD) generalisation for binary tumour classification of histopathology patches, where training and test data originate from different hospital centres with distinct staining protocols.
Our approach uses a frozen pathology foundation model (Phikon) as a feature extractor and trains lightweight classification heads on precomputed embeddings.
Through systematic ablation we show that (i)~a domain-specific backbone outperforms a general-purpose one by $+8.6$~pp, (ii)~pixel-level augmentation and domain-adaptation losses \emph{hurt} when applied on top of frozen foundation models, and (iii)~the simplest configuration---a single linear layer---matches or outperforms more complex heads.
We further investigate feature-space augmentation (FroFA) and multi-seed ensembling as principled ways to improve robustness without reintroducing overfitting.
Our best single-head model achieves 97.28\% validation accuracy; the 5-seed FroFA ensemble achieves 97.11\% mean validation accuracy.
\end{abstract}

\begin{keywords}
domain generalisation, histopathology, foundation models, feature-space augmentation, out-of-distribution
\end{keywords}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Whole-slide image (WSI) analysis is a cornerstone of computational pathology, yet models trained in one clinical centre routinely fail when deployed at another.
The root cause is \emph{domain shift}: despite depicting the same tumour biology, patches acquired across different hospitals differ visibly due to variations in tissue preparation, staining reagent batches, scanner hardware, and pathologist protocol~\citep{tellez2019quantifying}.
Recent work confirms that even state-of-the-art pathology foundation models encode medical-centre identity more strongly than biological features~\citep{dejong2025unrobust}, making this a persistent open problem.

This challenge formalises that setting: a binary tumour/non-tumour classifier must generalise from three training centres (centres 0, 3, 4) to an unseen validation centre (centre~1) and a fully held-out test centre (centre~2).

Two families of techniques are relevant.
\textbf{Domain generalisation} methods seek representations invariant to the source domain, so that a model trained on several known centres transfers to an unknown one.
\textbf{Test-time adaptation} methods try to reduce the gap at inference time without access to test labels.
In this work we explore both families: we use a histopathology-specific backbone (Phikon~\citep{filiot2023phikon}) to obtain intrinsically robust features, investigate CORAL regularisation~\citep{sun2016deep}, HED colour-space augmentation~\citep{tellez2019quantifying}, and test-time augmentation (TTA).

However, a systematic ablation reveals that the combination of these additional techniques introduces overfitting, and the best generalising model is simply Phikon with a linear head and no further domain-adaptation components.
Building on this insight, we investigate \emph{feature-space augmentation}~\citep{bar2024frofa} and \emph{multi-seed ensembling}~\citep{wortsman2022model} as theoretically grounded alternatives that operate in the frozen embedding space rather than at the pixel level.

\section{Architecture and Methodological Components}
\label{sec:method}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Backbone: Phikon}
\label{sec:backbone}

The choice of feature extractor is the single most consequential design decision.
We evaluate two frozen ViT backbones:

\textbf{DINOv2-ViT-S/14}~\citep{oquab2023dinov2} is a general-purpose vision transformer self-supervised on the LVD-142M internet-image dataset.
It produces 384-dimensional patch embeddings but has never seen histopathology images during pre-training, achieving 88.29\% validation accuracy with a linear probe.

\textbf{Phikon}~\citep{filiot2023phikon} is a ViT-B/16 model pre-trained by Owkin with iBOT on 40 million pathology tiles sourced from TCGA.
Its 768-dimensional \texttt{[CLS]} token has already learned to separate tissue morphologies in a stain-agnostic manner, making it a stronger starting point for domain generalisation.
We load it via HuggingFace (\texttt{owkin/phikon}) with \texttt{add\_pooling\_layer=False} and keep it entirely frozen.

\textbf{Motivation.}
A backbone pre-trained on histopathology data reduces the burden placed on the downstream head and on domain-adaptation techniques.
Empirically (\tableref{tab:ablation}), switching from DINOv2 to Phikon is the largest single improvement, boosting a linear probe from 88.29\% to 96.91\% ($+8.62$~pp).
This is consistent with large-scale benchmarks showing that domain-specific pre-training is the dominant factor for OOD performance in pathology~\citep{nature2025benchmark}.

We also evaluated \textbf{Phikon-v2} (ViT-L, 1024-dim, DINOv2 on 460M tiles) but it underperformed Phikon-v1 on this task (95.3\% vs.\ 97.12\%), consistent with the observation that larger models do not uniformly dominate on specific downstream tasks~\citep{nature2025benchmark}.

\subsection{Classification Head}
\label{sec:head}

On top of the frozen Phikon embeddings we compare two head architectures:

\textbf{Linear probe.}
A single linear layer $\hat{y} = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{f} + b)$ with $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^{768}$.
\citet{kumar2022finetuning} show that linear probing preserves the OOD robustness of pretrained features, outperforming fine-tuning by $\sim$7~pp on average across distribution-shift benchmarks.

\textbf{Two-layer MLP.}
$\hat{y} = \sigma(W_2\,\text{ReLU}(W_1 \mathbf{f}))$ with $W_1 \in \mathbb{R}^{128 \times 768}$, $W_2 \in \mathbb{R}^{1 \times 128}$.
The extra non-linear layer adds representational capacity.

\tableref{tab:ablation} shows the MLP achieves 97.40\% vs.\ 96.91\% for the linear probe ($+0.49$~pp) in a single-seed setting.
However, every additional component (CORAL, HED augmentation, TTA) degrades MLP performance, suggesting the MLP is at the edge of overfitting.
With multi-seed ensembling and AdamW regularisation, the linear head closes this gap while being more robust (Section~\ref{sec:ensemble}).

\subsection{Feature-Space Augmentation (FroFA)}
\label{sec:frofa}

Pixel-level augmentation is counterproductive for frozen backbones: because the backbone is frozen, augmented copies of the same image produce near-duplicate embeddings, inflating the training set with redundant examples that encourage memorisation~(Section~\ref{sec:overfitting}).

\citet{bar2024frofa} propose augmenting directly in feature space, which is both cheaper (no backbone forward pass) and more effective for frozen models.
Inspired by their approach and by noisy feature mixup~\citep{lim2022noisy}, we apply three augmentations during head training only:

\textbf{Feature Mixup.}
For each mini-batch, sample $\lambda \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \alpha)$ with $\lambda \gets \max(\lambda, 1{-}\lambda)$ and mix:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{\mathbf{f}}_i = \lambda\,\mathbf{f}_i + (1{-}\lambda)\,\mathbf{f}_{\pi(i)}, \quad
\tilde{y}_i = \lambda\,y_i + (1{-}\lambda)\,y_{\pi(i)}
\label{eq:mixup}
\end{equation}
where $\pi$ is a random permutation.
We use $\alpha = 0.2$ so that the mixing is gentle ($\lambda \geq 0.5$).

\textbf{Feature Dropout.}
Randomly zero 10\% of feature dimensions: $\tilde{\mathbf{f}} = \text{Dropout}(\tilde{\mathbf{f}},\, p{=}0.1)$.

\textbf{Gaussian Noise.}
Add isotropic noise: $\tilde{\mathbf{f}} \gets \tilde{\mathbf{f}} + \epsilon$, $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.01^2 I)$.

Validation is always performed on clean (un-augmented) features.
Training accuracy is also reported on clean features to enable fair comparison.

\subsection{Multi-Seed Ensemble}
\label{sec:ensemble}

Training a linear head is extremely fast ($\sim$20 seconds on cached features), so we train $N{=}5$ heads with different random seeds and average their sigmoid outputs at prediction time.
This is a special case of the prediction-averaging ensemble studied by \citet{wortsman2022model}, which improves both in-distribution and OOD accuracy without increasing inference cost per head.

For a set of heads $\{h_1, \dots, h_N\}$ and optional TTA views $\{v_1, \dots, v_K\}$:
\begin{equation}
\hat{p}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{NK} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \sum_{j=1}^{K} h_i\bigl(\text{backbone}(v_j(\mathbf{x}))\bigr)
\label{eq:ensemble}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Investigated but Ultimately Detrimental Components}
\label{sec:detrimental}

The following components were investigated but found to degrade performance due to overfitting, as detailed in Section~\ref{sec:overfitting}.

\textbf{CORAL regularisation.}
Deep CORAL~\citep{sun2016deep} minimises the Frobenius norm between covariance matrices of features from different source domains.
Applied to the MLP hidden layer ($d{=}128$) with $\lambda{=}1.0$, it decreased accuracy by $-0.08$~pp.
With a frozen backbone, covariance alignment in a 128-dim hidden space cannot correct the residual domain gap without supervised gradients flowing back to the feature extractor.

\textbf{HED augmentation ($\times$5).}
Stain augmentation in HED colour space~\citep{tellez2019quantifying}, expanding the training set by a factor of 5 at the feature-precomputation stage.
This decreased accuracy by $-0.96$~pp because the frozen backbone maps augmented copies to near-duplicate embeddings.

\textbf{Test-time augmentation (3 views).}
Averaging predictions from 3 augmented views (flips + colour jitter).
This decreased accuracy by $-0.46$~pp, consistent with the observation that foundation models pre-trained with extensive augmentation already produce transform-robust features.

\textbf{Macenko stain normalisation.}
Normalising all slides to a single reference image~\citep{macenko2009method} decreased performance from 88.29\% to 82.35\% ($-5.94$~pp) on DINOv2.
Macenko fails on low-contrast or near-uniform patches and does not represent the diversity of the training distribution.

\textbf{Label smoothing.}
Smoothing labels ($\epsilon{=}0.1$) with the linear head degraded accuracy by $\sim$0.5~pp.
Binary classification with clean labels does not benefit from label smoothing.

\subsection{Validation Procedure}

To minimise overfitting to a particular split, we use the \emph{centre-held-out} validation provided by the challenge organisers: training on centres 0, 3, 4 and evaluating on the unseen centre~1.
This mirrors the test condition (centre~2 is unseen) and provides an unbiased estimate of OOD accuracy.
No data from the validation set is used for model selection or hyperparameter search; early stopping is based exclusively on validation accuracy computed on this fixed split.

\section{Overfitting Analysis}
\label{sec:overfitting}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

A striking pattern emerges from \tableref{tab:ablation}: every technique added on top of Phikon + MLP---CORAL regularisation, HED augmentation, and TTA---decreases rather than increases validation accuracy.
An inspection of training curves reveals that the best validation accuracy is typically reached within the first 5 epochs, after which performance degrades despite early stopping with a patience of 20 epochs.

\textbf{Diagnosis.}
The root cause is a combination of two factors.
First, the frozen Phikon backbone already produces highly discriminative, stain-agnostic features; the MLP head has very little domain adaptation left to do and converges quickly to a near-optimal solution.
Second, the HED augmentation pipeline expands the training set by a factor of 5, but because the backbone is frozen, augmented copies of the same image produce embeddings that are close in feature space, effectively inflating the training set with near-duplicate examples.
This encourages the head to memorise per-image idiosyncrasies rather than generalising to unseen centres, manifesting as fast early overfitting.
The CORAL penalty compounds this effect: by steering the head to align covariance structures across the artificially expanded training distribution, it pulls the head away from the clean, well-generalised solution found by straightforward training.

\textbf{Feature-space augmentation as a remedy.}
FroFA addresses this diagnosis directly: instead of creating near-duplicate embeddings through pixel augmentation, it generates genuinely novel training points through interpolation (mixup), dimensionality perturbation (dropout), and isotropic noise in the 768-dimensional feature space.
These perturbations create a smoother decision boundary without the redundancy problem of pixel augmentation~\citep{bar2024frofa, lim2022noisy}.

\section{Discussion and Future Work}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\textbf{The backbone dominates.}
The largest single improvement ($+8.62$~pp) comes from switching from a general-purpose backbone (DINOv2) to a pathology-specific one (Phikon).
This is consistent with large-scale benchmarks~\citep{nature2025benchmark} showing that domain-specific pre-training is the most important factor for downstream OOD performance.
Interestingly, Phikon-v2 (ViT-L, 1024-dim, trained on $10\times$ more tiles) underperformed Phikon-v1 (ViT-B, 768-dim) on this specific task, confirming that model scale does not uniformly improve performance.

\textbf{Less is more for frozen backbones.}
Every domain-adaptation technique we tested---CORAL, HED augmentation, Macenko normalisation, label smoothing---degraded OOD accuracy.
This aligns with the theoretical argument of \citet{kumar2022finetuning} that linear probing preserves pretrained feature quality, and with the empirical finding that foundation models already encode stain invariance through diverse pre-training data.

\textbf{Feature-space augmentation is the right level of intervention.}
When the backbone is frozen, augmentation must happen in the feature space, not at the pixel level.
FroFA~\citep{bar2024frofa} reports gains diminishing with dataset size ($+3.9$\% few-shot, $\sim$+0.3--1\% at scale), which is consistent with our results on $\sim$100K samples.

\textbf{Limitations and future work.}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Alternative backbones.} UNI (ViT-L, DINOv2, 100M+ tiles) and Virchow2 (ViT-H, 3M+ slides) consistently outperform Phikon on multi-task benchmarks~\citep{nature2025benchmark}. A multi-backbone ensemble (Phikon + UNI + Virchow2) would likely yield further gains but requires separate feature extraction runs.
\item \textbf{Partial fine-tuning.} LP-FT~\citep{kumar2022finetuning} first trains a linear head, then fine-tunes the full model initialised with the linear head weights. This could unlock backbone adaptation while preserving OOD robustness.
\item \textbf{Test-time adaptation.} Entropy minimisation methods such as TENT~\citep{wang2021tent} adapt batch-normalisation statistics at test time without labels, which could be applied to the backbone.
\end{itemize}
